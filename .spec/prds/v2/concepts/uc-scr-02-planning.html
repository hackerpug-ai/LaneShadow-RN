§01 · Purpose

Transparency is non-negotiable when a machine is choosing.

PlanningScreen is the inside of the Navigator's head. The rider has asked for a ride; the system is now scouting, drawing, weather-checking, scoring, and scenic-ranking. None of that happens behind a blank spinner. The phase indicator — compass chip, italic headline, five steps with a pulsing active ring — is a promise to the rider that the thinking is legible.

The sketching polyline is the map doing the same job. Instead of a static "loading" overlay, the paper shows the Navigator's pen moving — a dashed copper stroke with a breathing leading dot, looping until the result arrives. The rider knows roughly where the answer will land before it lands.

The chat input is deliberately inert. Typing is disabled; the send button is replaced by a spinner; the rider's prompt stays visible so they can remember what they asked for. The only way out is back, which pops the planning stack and returns to Idle.

§06 · Rationale

Why these five steps, not one.

Design principles honored

Blank spinners lie. A route isn't arriving in 200ms; it's being composed over 3–8 seconds of genuine reasoning. If the rider has to wait, they deserve to know roughly where in the pipeline their request is. The five steps aren't decorative — they map 1:1 to the Navigator's LangGraph node execution.

The sketching polyline is the soul of this screen. It converts a technical progress signal into a legible, paper-map metaphor. By the time the rider sees the final routes, their eye already knows the shape of the answer.
