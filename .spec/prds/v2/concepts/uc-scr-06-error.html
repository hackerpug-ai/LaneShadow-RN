§01 · Purpose

Failure as a conversation, not a dead end.

ErrorScreen is the Navigator's humility surface. When the planner can't produce a route that meets the rider's constraints — a segment was broken, an endpoint was unreachable, a network call timed out, a safety rule tripped — the screen doesn't blank out or modal-slam. It drops an inline callout where the Navigator message would normally sit, explains what went wrong in plain Newsreader prose, and offers two suggestion chips the rider can tap to try an adjusted version of the same ask.

The distinction between this screen and an error toast is structural: toasts auto-dismiss; this callout stays. It uses the same LSGlassPanel callout chassis as UC-SCR-03's NavigatorMessage — same compass chip, same label row, same opinion-serif body — but swaps the top-stripe from signal-copper to status-warning, and the compass chip to a warning tint. Visual continuity is on purpose: the Navigator is still speaking, just honestly.

The chat input remains fully active and primed with a recovery placeholder ("Try again, or let me know what to change…"). The rider always has three doors out: (1) tap a suggestion chip, (2) rewrite the prompt in the input, or (3) tap the map to dismiss the callout and pan back to Idle.

§06 · Rationale

Why warn, not error.

Design principles honored

The screen uses warning (copper) as the primary accent — not error (red). A failed plan is not a broken app; it's a conversation that didn't quite land. Warning keeps the Navigator's voice intact. Error-red is reserved for the map's broken-segment markers and crossed-out attempted paths, where the point is spatial: this specific edge cannot be traversed, not the app is sorry.

Suggestion chips are opinions, not form fields. Two primary suggestions (warning-tinted) are the Navigator's best guesses at what to try next; a third tertiary "Rewrite the ask" is the escape hatch that always exists. This three-way structure keeps the rider from feeling cornered.
