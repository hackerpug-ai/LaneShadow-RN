Setting sun, locked. Now: how do we frame it?

The setting-sun image is settled. But the same elements (sun, horizon, road) make three structurally different logos depending on how much "scene" we put around them and whether we wrap them in type. Each format takes a different position in the marketplace and against the philosophy.

A — The Pure Icon

Three elements. No frame. No type. Just the moment.

What it is: sun, horizon, road. Bauhaus-economy composition. Three primary forms: disc, line, triangle. Total ink budget: minimal. The whole thing reads in a third of a second.

Position: modern, confident, restrained. Like a Sagi Haviv mark. Says "we don't need to explain ourselves."

Pros: bulletproof at any size (16px favicon → 1024px). Highest "iconic potential" of the three. Endures decades.

Cons: non-rider sees a sun with a road and might think travel, gas station, or trucking. Loses some "for motorcycles specifically" specificity unless paired with strong wordmark.

B — The Illustrated Medallion

A small landscape. The road, the sun, the place — and you, tiny in the distance.

What it is: the same scene plus subtle context — a low silhouette of distant mesas/hills, a slight curve to the road, and a barely-visible tiny rider near the vanishing point. Hand-inked, warm-paper, the texture a Sibley field plate has.

Position: literary, illustrated, specific. Like a small WPA park-poster medallion or a Field Notes "Two Rivers" cover stamp. Says "there's a story here, and someone has been there."

Pros: highest emotional resonance — the tiny rider plus the big landscape is the romance of riding distilled. Most LaneShadow-specific (other apps cannot easily steal this register). Strongest fit with the existing philosophy doc.

Cons: at 16px the mesa silhouette and rider collapse. Probably needs a "small-scale variant" that drops the rider and simplifies the mesa to a single horizon ridge.

C — The Heritage Badge

The patch. The seal. The official-looking thing a rider sews on.

What it is: the setting-sun scene wrapped in a double-ring seal with arc-text wordmark on top (LANESHADOW) and tagline on bottom (RIDE THE MOMENT). Hand-set serif type, slight imperfection, the format of a 1933 WPA park badge or an Iron Butt Association patch.

Position: heritage, club, instantly legible. Says "this is a real thing for real riders, and we've been here a while." The wordmark is built in — no separate type pairing needed.

Pros: highest "first-impression communication" — even a non-rider knows in 0.5 seconds this is a riders' brand because of the patch format. Wordmark integrated. Most merchandise-ready (this IS already a sticker, a t-shirt, a patch).

Cons: the philosophy doc explicitly named "letterform-first marks" as a rejection — the badge is the closest sibling to that and we'd be partially walking it back. Two tiers (full badge + simplified favicon) needed because arc-text disappears below ~96px.

My recommendation

A 2-into-1 system: B (Illustrated Medallion) as the primary mark and C (Heritage Badge) as a related secondary lockup — same scene at the heart, badge format added when context calls for it (merch, t-shirts, patches, marketing posters). The badge wraps the medallion. They're not competing concepts; they're tier 1 and tier 2 of the same identity. The pure icon (A) becomes the favicon-tier simplification — what the medallion collapses into below 32px. We get all three on a continuum: 16px = A, app-icon = B, t-shirt = C.

What I'm asking

Pick a primary direction (click), or affirm the 2-into-1 system, or push back. If "B as primary, C as secondary, A as favicon" feels right, just type that. If you want one to win outright, name it. If something is missing — like "I want it darker / more vintage / less polished" — say so before we commit pen to paper.
